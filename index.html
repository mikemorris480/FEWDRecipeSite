Positively, Absolutely, Not Real Bouillabaisse

Alton Brown's "Positively, Absolutely, Not Real Bouillabaisse" is a playful twist on the classic French seafood stew. While it captures the essence of bouillabaisse with its rich fish stock, aromatic saffron, and a medley of seafood like mussels, lobster, and various fish fillets, it deliberately deviates from tradition. The key distinction lies in the absence of rascasse, the scorpion fish that is a hallmark of authentic bouillabaisse. This omission is what makes Alton's version "not real," as rascasse is essential to the dish's identity and flavor profile. Instead, Alton's recipe embraces creativity, offering a delicious and approachable alternative for those who want to enjoy the spirit of bouillabaisse without adhering strictly to its traditional roots.

  • Level: Advanced
  • Total: 2 hours
  • Prep: 40 min
  • Inactive: 5 min
  • Cook: 1 hr 15 min
  • Yield: 6 to 8 Servings

Ingredients

  • 1 1/2 pounds white fish fillets (such as snapper, grouper, or cod), cut into 2-inch pieces
  • 1 1/2 pounds mussels, scrubbed and debearded
  • 1 1/2 pounds lobster tails, split
  • 1/2 cup olive oil
  • 1 large onion, chopped
  • 1 fennel bulb, chopped
  • 1 leek, white part only, chopped

Directions

  1. Place the fish, mussels, and lobster in separate bowls; refrigerate until needed.
  2. Heat the olive oil in a large stockpot over medium heat. Add the onion, fennel, and leek and cook until soft, about 5 minutes.
  3. Add the fish bones and heads, shrimp shells, and tomato paste. Cook, stirring, for 5 minutes.
  4. Add the wine, tomatoes, saffron, and water. Bring to a boil, then reduce the heat and simmer for 1 hour.
  5. Strain the stock through a fine-mesh sieve into a clean pot. Discard the solids.
  6. Bring the stock to a simmer. Add the fish, mussels, and lobster and cook until the fish is opaque and the mussels have opened, about 5 minutes.
  7. Season with salt and pepper. Serve hot with crusty bread.

Notes

This recipe is a playful twist on the classic French seafood stew bouillabaisse. While it captures the essence of the dish, it deliberately deviates from tradition by omitting the essential ingredient rascasse. This omission is what makes Alton Brown's version "not real," offering a creative and approachable alternative for those who want to enjoy the spirit of bouillabaisse without adhering strictly to its traditional roots.
